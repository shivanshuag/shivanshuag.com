---
title: 'Back to the roots'
description: 'Why I am starting to write and build in the open again — finding my way back to the software community that first made me fall in love with this.'
pubDate: 2026-07-23
tags: ['personal', 'community', 'writing']
---

I got into building software early in my university days. Building something feels empowering in a way few things do — you have an idea in your head, and then it exists.

At that time, it was a way to satisfy intellectual curiosity, or just to prove to myself that I could make something cool. I thrived in a community of like-minded people. And through them I discovered a much larger one online — social media, forums, open-source mailing lists, IRC — and felt like part of it. The breadth of ideas moving through that constant back-and-forth was something I'd never seen before. People building in the open, sharing half-finished thoughts, arguing, helping strangers debug things at odd hours. It felt alive.

Then I graduated and started my career as a software engineer. The corporate environment is different. It has taught me how to build software predictably and reliably — with real rigor, at a scale I couldn't have reached on my own. I'm still innovating, still working with great people, some of them genuine industry leaders. I've learned an enormous amount, and I don't regret it.

But somewhere along the way, something started to feel missing.

The work happens inside walls. The problems I solve, the things I learn, the ideas I have — they mostly stay within the boundaries of the company. Conversations happen in private channels; context lives in internal docs; what I build is rarely something I can point to and say "look at this." Over time that gets quietly isolating. You can be surrounded by talented people and still feel like you've stopped being part of the larger conversation — the one that first made me fall in love with this.

I've realized the way out is the thing I stopped doing: sharing, and communicating with the world again. Building in the open. Being part of the software community outside of a workplace, the way I was at the start.

So that's what this is. I'm going to start writing — about systems and databases, the things I'm learning and building, the ideas I can't stop thinking about. Some of it will be rough. Some of it will probably be wrong, and I'm hoping someone tells me so. That's the whole point. This is me finding my way back to the roots — and hoping to meet a few of you along the way.
